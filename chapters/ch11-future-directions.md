# Chapter 11. Future Directions

This chapter applies the survey's evidence discipline to the future. Every claim below carries its Charter class—shipping (A), announced (B), publicly disclosed development (C), credibly reported (D), speculative (E)—and the chapter is ordered by descending evidence, so that the reader always knows when the ground shifts from schedule to research to conjecture. Two rules govern the analysis. First, futures are evaluated with the same instruments as the present: a candidate technology must name the residual bound it attacks (Chapter 2), the contract it changes (Chapter 3), and its path through the necessity test's fourth condition (§2.14); many technologies have satisfied conditions 1–3 and still failed at condition 4. Second, per §2.15, the workload future is itself uncertain; a hardware future that assumes today's model families inherits that assumption's risk.

## 11.1 The Scheduled Future (Classes A/B)

Some of the future is simply on the roadmap, and it extends trajectories this survey has already analyzed.

- **Memory**: HBM4 in mass production with extended variants following [10.1], driving the Figure 7.1 capacity race through the decade's second half.
- **Fabrics**: UALink and Ultra Ethernet silicon arriving [10.2][10.3], testing whether scale-up can be commoditized.
- **Optics**: co-packaged optics in switches shipping now [10.4], with package-level optical I/O the disclosed next step (class B/C).
- **Precision**: FP4 and block-scaled microscaling formats becoming the default inference format, continuing the §3.6 narrow-precision trajectory toward its numerical floor.
- **Packaging**: chiplet composition standardizing (UCIe, UALink chiplet spec) toward vendor-mixed sockets (class B).

None of this changes the survey's structure; all of it intensifies the convergence Chapter 7 documented, with the industry attacking the memory and communication bounds in parallel. The futures that would change a *contract* are the analytically interesting ones, and they follow.

## 11.2 Processing-in-Memory: The Nearest Contract Change (Classes B/C)

Chapter 6 (§6.8) left PIM gated not by architecture but by programming models and memory-standards economics. The credible path forward runs through standardization: JEDEC-track PIM commands would convert bespoke integrations into a portable target, at which point the §2.6 embedding and §2.5 streaming bounds—both measured, both growing—supply immediate demand. The realistic near-term form is conservative: fixed-function reduction and gather primitives in HBM base dies and LPDDR variants (class B/C), not general in-memory compute. The survey's watch item: the first mainstream accelerator whose *datasheet* advertises in-memory operations as part of the platform contract, which would mark the bound's absorption into the incumbent trajectory per §5.11—likely the way this family finally ships at volume.

## 11.3 Photonic Computing (Classes C/E)

Photonics must be split per the Charter's taxonomy discipline into three propositions with different verdicts. **Photonic interconnect** is class A/B and covered (§10.3): the supply chain is being built now. **Photonic switching of electronic compute** (optical circuit switching between pods) is class A at one hyperscaler [2.24] and diffusing. **Photonic computation**—optical matrix multiplication through interferometer meshes or wavelength multiplexing—remains class C/E: laboratory and startup demonstrations show the physics (a passive optical mesh multiplies at propagation speed and near-zero marginal energy), while the system problems remain unsolved at competitive scale: conversion overhead at the electronic boundary (the ADC/DAC energy repeatedly exceeds the optical savings—§4.2's boundary-accounting rule applied to light), limited effective precision, and no story for the non-matrix majority of the graph. The necessity-test reading: photonic compute currently fails condition 3 at the system boundary—the lever changes a bound that conversion costs restore—and its plausible entry point is therefore hybrid: optical operators embedded inside an electronic platform, arriving through the packaging path §10.3 is already building.

## 11.4 Analog In-Memory Computing (Class C)

Analog crossbars—storing weights as conductances, computing matrix-vector products by physics (Ohm's and Kirchhoff's laws)—attack the same §2.5 weight-movement bound as §6.4 and §6.8, with research-grade silicon demonstrating transformer-relevant workloads at striking projected efficiencies [11.1]. The unresolved costs are the family's defining tradeoffs: device drift and variability consuming the numerical contract (§3.6) through calibration and redundancy; ADC overhead dominating energy at required precisions (the same boundary tax as photonics); and write costs constraining which tensors can live in the array (weights yes, KV state no—which maps the family onto inference of stable models, §6.4's envelope). Class C throughout; the survey's assessment is that analog's likeliest commercial form mirrors PIM's: bounded analog tiles inside digital platforms for the always-on and edge tiers first (§6.5's envelope, where precision demands are lowest and energy value is highest), rather than a datacenter assault.

## 11.5 Spintronics, MRAM, and Post-CMOS Devices (Classes B–E)

The device-level future divides cleanly. **Embedded MRAM** is class A/B as storage: nonvolatile on-chip weight memory at microcontroller scale directly serves §6.5's instant-on, zero-standby envelope and is in foundry processes now. **Spintronic and other post-CMOS logic** (magnetic, ferroelectric, tunnel-FET devices) remains class E for computation: laboratory switching demonstrations without credible system paths inside this survey's horizon. The honest summary for architects: expect new *memories* inside conventional architectures well before new *logic*, because a memory technology can enter through one tier of the §3.5 hierarchy without renegotiating every contract, while a logic technology must beat CMOS plus its entire ecosystem at once—the hardest condition-4 problem, a point this survey has established several times.

## 11.6 Neuromorphic and Event-Driven Trajectories (Classes C/E)

Section 6.9's assessment stands and extends: the family's fortunes track the emergence of natively event-based workloads rather than any silicon milestone. Two developments would change its class: a training pipeline that produces spiking deployments from mainstream tooling without accuracy loss (the §9.5 coverage problem in its hardest form), or robotic and wearable sensor suites adopting event cameras and continuous biosignals at volume—supplying the sparse-in-time signals the architecture is built for (§8.4's small-robot tier is the plausible beachhead). Absent those, neuromorphic remains a reminder that activity-proportional energy is the theoretical endpoint of the wake-scope logic (§2.10): a direction of development rather than a realized destination.

## 11.7 Workload Wildcards: The Demand Side of the Future

The supply-side futures above all assume Chapter 2's signature landscape; the demand side may move first, and §2.15's model-independence discipline applies here. Three shifts with hardware consequences are already visible in class A/B form. **Test-time compute scaling**—models that reason longer rather than grow larger—moves cost from training to inference and deepens the decode bound's dominance, strengthening every §6.4/§6.8 thesis and the inference-first silicon trend (§7.2). **Persistent-state agents and world models** (§2.15) would promote state residency, freshness, and controlled mutation to first-class bounds—territory none of today's families owns cleanly, and the most plausible source of a genuinely new family this survey would need in a future edition. **Structured sparsity and modularity** (MoE as default, retrieval as memory) keep pushing the routing and gather signatures (§2.6, §2.7) up the priority list, where the memory-centric and fabric futures above are waiting. The survey's own method predicts its revision: if the workload signatures move, the family landscape follows within a hardware generation.

## 11.8 Quantum Computing: An Acknowledgment

Per the Charter, quantum computing is outside this survey's scope, and the exclusion is analytical, not dismissive: quantum acceleration addresses computational classes (simulation of quantum systems, certain optimization and cryptanalytic structures) that do not intersect the dense-linear-algebra and memory-bound signatures this survey characterizes, and no credible path connects current quantum hardware to mainstream AI serving economics inside this edition's horizon. A future edition should revisit the question if either quantum-native ML algorithms with demonstrated advantage or hybrid quantum-classical workflows with production evidence emerge; the evidence discipline required is the same one this chapter has applied throughout.

## 11.9 Engineering Takeaways

- The scheduled future (HBM4, CPO, UALink/UEC, FP4, chiplets) intensifies the present rather than changing it: the industry will spend the rest of the decade attacking the same memory and communication bounds this survey measured, with the contracts intact.
- The nearest genuine contract changes—PIM primitives and analog tiles—will most likely arrive *inside* incumbent platforms via standardization and packaging rather than as standalone families, repeating the absorption pattern of §5.11.
- Photonics enters through interconnect, not computation: the conversion-boundary tax currently defeats optical compute's condition 3, while co-packaged optics builds the supply chain any future optical computing would need.
- Expect new memories before new logic: technologies that enter through one memory tier (MRAM, PIM) face a tractable condition 4, while post-CMOS logic must defeat the entire CMOS ecosystem at once and remains speculative within this edition's horizon.
- The demand side may move first: test-time compute scaling, persistent-state agents, and default sparsity would redistribute the bound landscape—deepening decode dominance and elevating state residency—and the family taxonomy should be re-derived from measured signatures at each edition, per §2.15.
- Every future candidate should be interrogated with the same three questions as any present system: which measured bound, which changed contract, and what answer to condition 4—the record shows the third question is where futures are decided.
