# Preface

Artificial intelligence has driven two major transitions in computing architecture. The first moved performance-critical work from general-purpose CPUs to highly parallel GPUs after software techniques such as vectorization, multithreading, cache blocking, and optimized numerical libraries encountered limits in throughput, memory bandwidth, and energy efficiency. The second transition is now underway: increasingly sophisticated GPU software—including kernel fusion, quantization, sparsity support, graph compilation, and communication overlap—continues to expose limitations in utilization, data movement, irregular execution, latency, scaling, and power. These pressures are producing a broader range of domain-specific accelerators rather than a single successor to the GPU.

This study explains those transitions from a systems-architecture perspective. Its primary goal is to connect the characteristics of modern AI workloads to software-visible bottlenecks, architectural responses, and real commercial implementations. It examines dense and sparse training, fine-tuning, prefill and autoregressive decode, recommendation, retrieval, mixture-of-experts models, vision, diffusion, multimodal systems, scientific AI, and real-time inference. The analysis emphasizes practical concerns—including locality, caching, memory hierarchy, communication, synchronization, load balance, determinism, energy, thermals, and cost—rather than peak arithmetic performance alone.

The study treats AI offload as a continuum spanning hyperscale datacenters, enterprise and edge servers, workstations, personal computers, phones, embedded systems, automotive platforms, and robotics. Architectural ideas and software practices move in both directions across this continuum, although each deployment class imposes different constraints. Products are therefore examined as implementations of architectural choices, not as an isolated vendor catalog.

The chapters deliver a workload taxonomy, a framework for evaluating architectural stressors and tradeoffs, a classification of accelerator families, and comparative studies of datacenter, client, and edge implementations. They also cover the supporting software ecosystem, memory and interconnect technologies, packaging, deployment economics, and credible future directions. Shipping products, announced systems, development programs, and speculative efforts are identified separately. Formal performance methods, terminology, detailed product tables, and selected microarchitectural material are retained as appendices so that the main text remains useful to senior software engineers, systems architects, and technically grounded decision makers.
