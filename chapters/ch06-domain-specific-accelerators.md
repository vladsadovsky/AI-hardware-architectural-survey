# Chapter 6. Domain-Specific AI Accelerators Across the Compute Continuum

Chapters 2 through 5 built the machinery this chapter now uses. Chapter 2 characterized workloads as signatures and executed the necessity test on real systems (§2.14). Chapter 3 defined the six contracts through which any accelerator must be evaluated. Chapter 4 priced the stressors and tradeoffs. Chapter 5 established the CPU–GPU baseline and partitioned the residual bounds into those the GPU platform absorbs, those structurally outside its contract, and a contested middle (§5.11). This chapter examines the architecture families that occupy the territory beyond that baseline—not as a catalog of devices, but as distinct, coherent answers to the design questions of §4.11.

Each family is a bundle of contract changes. A family earns its place in this chapter by changing at least one of Chapter 3’s contracts in a way the GPU platform cannot adopt without ceasing to be itself: replacing dynamic scheduling with compiler-owned time, replacing cache hierarchies with distributed explicit SRAM, replacing a central device with computation at the memory or the sensor, or replacing clocked dense arithmetic with event-driven sparsity. For every family, the question is never whether it can execute AI workloads—almost all can—but which workload signatures fall inside its efficient envelope, what its contract changes cost in software and lifecycle terms, and where it sits in the §5.11 partition.

A note on classification discipline. Following the taxonomy principles of the Charter, the families below are **microarchitectural organizations**, not execution models, dataflow strategies, or vendor categories; one physical product frequently combines several (a mobile SoC may contain an NPU built around a systolic array, programmed as a spatial dataflow, marketed under a proprietary name). Vendor labels are used only to name exemplars, never to classify. Where a family’s published numbers are quoted, the comparison level (die, package, socket, wafer, node, or system) is stated explicitly, and evidence classes follow the Charter: shipping and verified (A), officially announced (B), publicly disclosed development (C), credibly reported (D), speculative (E).

## 6.1 How to Read an Architecture Family

Every family section below follows one template, so that differences between families are visible as differences in answers rather than differences in presentation:

- **Primer.** One to three paragraphs of the essential mechanism—what is physically different from the CPU–GPU baseline, written so that a software architect can immediately locate the novelty.
- **The contracts, rewritten.** Which of the six contracts (execution, data placement, numerical, scheduling, communication, software) the family changes, and what each change transfers into software.
- **Evidence anchors.** Named exemplar systems with published numbers, evidence classes, and explicit comparison levels. Product-against-product comparison is deferred to Chapter 7; exemplars here are existence proofs of the architectural choices.
- **Envelope.** The workload signatures (Chapter 2) inside and outside the family’s efficiency envelope, with strengths and weaknesses stated against measurement boundaries.
- **Continuum.** Where the family lives across phone → embedded → workstation → edge → enterprise → hyperscale deployment, per the Charter’s continuum mandate.
- **Position and risk.** The family’s place in the §5.11 partition, and which condition of the §2.14 necessity test is most at risk for it.

Three reading rules prevent common category errors. First, a family’s peak arithmetic rating is its least informative property; the interesting properties are always where state lives, who schedules time, and what happens to unsupported operations. Second, no family is evaluated against the GPU of its design year; the correct comparison is against the GPU trajectory plus software capital (§5.11), which is the competitor it actually faces. Third, the same family instantiates differently at different points of the continuum—an NPU in a phone and a tensor processor in a hyperscale pod share ancestry and dataflow ideas but answer different binding constraints, and the sections below keep those design points distinct.

## 6.2 Systolic Arrays

### Primer

A systolic array is a grid of small processing elements, each holding a few operands and an accumulator, connected only to its neighbors. Data enters at the array’s edges and advances one hop per cycle in a fixed rhythm—the “heartbeat” that gave the structure its name [3.1]. In the weight-stationary form most common in AI hardware, each processing element holds one weight; activations stream horizontally, partial sums stream vertically, and every value fetched from memory is reused at every element it passes. There is no instruction fetch per operation, no address generation per operand, no cache lookup, and no arbitration: the geometry of the array *is* the schedule.

For a software architect the essential novelty is this: in a CPU or GPU, every multiply-accumulate is accompanied by machinery that finds it, names its operands, and routes its result—machinery that Chapter 5 showed consuming most of the area and energy. In a systolic array, that machinery is executed once, at design time, for the entire structure. The cost per operation collapses toward the cost of the arithmetic itself, at the price of accepting a fixed communication geometry that only some computations fit.

### The contracts, rewritten

The systolic array rewrites the execution and data-placement contracts jointly. Execution becomes positional: an operation happens because data arrives, not because an instruction issues. Data placement becomes geometric: reuse is created by the path a value takes through the array, not discovered by a cache. The scheduling contract moves almost entirely to the compiler or the surrounding control logic, which must tile tensors into array-shaped passes, sequence weight loads, and keep edge buffers fed. The communication contract shrinks to nearest-neighbor links—extremely cheap, but expressive only for computations whose data dependencies are local and regular, which dense matrix multiplication and convolution are, and sparse gather, routing, and general graph aggregation are not.

What transfers into software is shape responsibility. An array of dimension N×N is fully productive only when the mapped tile occupies its rows and columns; narrow matrices—precisely the batch-one decode shapes of §2.5—leave columns idle. Chapter 3’s mapping vocabulary (§3.4) applies directly: the array fixes a dataflow, and the compiler’s tiling, folding, and padding decisions determine how much of the geometric reuse is realized.

### A worked anchor: reuse from geometry

The magnitude of the reuse deserves one author-computed illustration. TPU v1’s 256×256 weight-stationary array performs 65,536 multiply-accumulates per cycle at 700 MHz—roughly 92 tera-operations per second at 8-bit precision [1.12]. Served naively—every operand fetched from memory for every operation—that rate would demand on the order of 200 TB/s of operand traffic (two 1-byte inputs per operation, before accumulator traffic). The actual design fed the array through a 24 MiB unified buffer and a weight FIFO from off-chip DRAM at bandwidths three to four orders of magnitude lower. The difference is not a memory technology; it is geometry. Each activation entering an edge is used 256 times as it crosses the array; each weight, once resident, is used for every activation streamed past it; each partial sum accumulates in place. The array converts a memory-bandwidth problem into a wiring pattern—which is why some form of systolic or semi-systolic structure appears inside nearly every dense-matrix engine in this chapter, including the GPU tensor cores of §5.5.

### Evidence anchors

The systolic array is a mechanism family; its exemplars are mostly the devices of later sections that embed it. TPU v1 is the canonical production instance (class A, §2.14) [1.12]. The Eyeriss research line demonstrated the dataflow-taxonomy analysis—weight-, output-, and row-stationary variants under common area constraints—that made systolic design choices comparable rather than anecdotal [1.10]. GPU tensor cores are the in-platform adoption of the same reuse geometry at small tile granularity inside a SIMT machine [5.8], the clearest single example of §5.11 absorption.

### Envelope

Inside the envelope: dense matrix multiplication, convolution (lowered or directly mapped), and any operator expressible as large regular contractions—dense training and prefill signatures of §2.3 and §2.5, vision backbones of §2.8. Outside: unstructured sparsity (zeros occupy array positions and destroy the reuse arithmetic), data-dependent routing, narrow matrix-vector shapes at small batch, and any computation whose dependency structure is not locally regular. The weaknesses are not incidental; they are the exact price of removing the machinery whose absence makes the structure efficient. Structured sparsity at fixed patterns can be accommodated by modified arrays; arbitrary sparsity cannot, which is why sparse workloads escalate to the differently organized families of §6.4 and §6.8.

### Continuum

The mechanism scales across the entire continuum by resizing: 256×256 arrays in datacenter tensor processors, tens-by-tens arrays in mobile NPUs, single-digit arrays in microNPUs beside microcontrollers [1.13]. Because the mechanism is geometry rather than a device, it migrates as parameterized IP in exactly the sense of §2.16—the same reuse principle at every scale, with array dimensions chosen against the deployment’s tile-size distribution and power envelope.

### Position and risk

The systolic mechanism itself is not contested territory: it has been absorbed by every side, including the GPU. What remains distinctive is the *scale* at which a device commits to it—an entire die organized around one large array (§6.3) versus many small arrays inside a programmable machine. The §2.14 risk for large-array commitment is condition 1 drift: if workload shape distributions move toward narrow, dynamic, or sparse operations, a large fixed array loses the utilization that justified its area. That is a workload-stability risk, and it is the recurring theme of the next section.

## 6.3 Tensor Processors

### Primer

A tensor processor is a device organized around one or a few large matrix engines—usually systolic or semi-systolic—fed by software-managed on-chip memory, explicit DMA, and a co-designed compiler, with the dynamic machinery of a CPU or GPU largely deleted. Where a GPU is a sea of general execution lanes with matrix units embedded in it, a tensor processor inverts the proportions: the matrix engine is the chip, and a thin layer of vector and scalar capability exists to serve it. Scheduling is predominantly static—the compiler, not the hardware, decides what happens when—which is what allowed TPU v1 to offer deterministic latency (§2.14) and what makes the family’s efficiency claims credible: area and energy not spent on discovery are spent on arithmetic and SRAM.

The second defining commitment, visible from TPU v2 onward, is that the *interconnect is part of the architecture*. Training-capable tensor processors are designed as pods: devices with dedicated high-bandwidth links arranged in tori or reconfigurable topologies, with collectives treated as first-class operations by the compiler [6.1][2.24]. A software architect should read this family as a vertically integrated stack—model, compiler, chip, and network co-designed by one organization—rather than as a chip alone. That integration is simultaneously the family’s greatest strength and its adoption barrier.

### The contracts, rewritten

Execution: wide matrix operations issued under compiler control replace dynamically scheduled thread groups; the ready-frontier problem of §3.3 is solved by construction, by scheduling only what the compiler proved ready. Data placement: software-managed SRAM buffers replace cache hierarchies; residency is a compiler decision, and Chapter 3’s scratchpad contract (§3.5, Table 3.3) applies in full. Numerical: aggressive narrow-precision formats with wide accumulation, co-designed with the owner’s training recipes—BF16 originated in this family’s lineage [3.5]. Scheduling: static at device level, dynamic only at pod and fleet level. Communication: dedicated inter-chip fabrics with hardware or compiler-managed collectives; TPU v4’s optically reconfigurable topology extends the principle to circuit-switched pod reconfiguration [2.24]. Software: a compiler stack (XLA in the TPU case) that must lower whole graphs; fallback does not cross to a co-resident general engine but to the host, making operator coverage a sharper cliff than on a GPU.

### Evidence anchors

TPU v1 established the economics (§2.14, class A) [1.12]. The TPUv2/v3 design retrospective documents the transition from inference to training: adding HBM, wider numerics, and the inter-chip torus that made the pod the unit of design [6.1]. TPU v4 (class A, vendor-published evaluation) reports a 4,096-chip system whose optical circuit switches cost under 5% of system cost and under 3% of system power, mean chip power near 200 W, roughly 2.7× performance-per-watt over TPU v3, and 1.2–1.7× the speed of similarly sized A100 systems at 1.3–1.9× less power on the reported workloads [2.24]—vendor-published measurements over the vendor’s own workload portfolio, contested by the GPU vendor at the time, and quoted here with that boundary explicit. AWS Trainium and Inferentia [1.18] and Meta’s MTIA [1.17] are class-A evidence that the family generalizes across hyperscalers whenever §2.14’s condition 4 is satisfied by internal volume. At the small end of the continuum, Edge TPU-class devices instantiate the same architectural ideas at single-watt scale [2.17].

### Envelope

The envelope is Chapter 2’s dense high-intensity region: large-batch training, prefill, vision backbones, and any portfolio dominated by large regular contractions with predictable shapes. Within it, the family’s deleted machinery is pure profit. The envelope’s edges are equally clear. Narrow dynamic shapes strand the big arrays (§6.2’s risk, inherited at device scale). Operators outside compiler coverage pay host-fallback penalties rather than co-resident SIMT penalties. Rapidly changing model families stress the co-designed stack: every architectural novelty must clear the compiler before it clears the hardware. The family’s answer to sparse and irregular signatures has been incremental—embedding-specific hardware such as TPU v4’s SparseCores [2.24] rather than general irregularity support—which is precisely what the workload-first method predicts: attack the specific production signature (recommendation embeddings, §2.6), not irregularity in the abstract.

### Continuum

Hyperscale is the family’s home: condition 4 of the necessity test is satisfied by internal workload volume, and the vertical stack is an asset rather than a barrier because one organization owns models, compiler, and fleet. Enterprise deployment is the family’s weakest point for exactly the reasons §2.14’s third case established—external customers inherit the stack without the internal volume—though cloud exposure of pods converts the ownership problem into a rental one. At the embedded and client end, the same organizational logic reappears in miniature: Edge TPU-class and NPU designs (§6.5) are tensor processors resized for single-model, single-user signatures, where the narrow compiler is less of a liability because the deployed model set is small and controlled.

### Position and risk

In the §5.11 partition the family is the strongest occupant of the contested middle’s dense-training-and-serving territory, and at hyperscale volume it has repeatedly cleared the necessity test—multiple generations, multiple owners, class-A deployments. Its exposed condition is workload stability (condition 4’s stability term): each generation freezes assumptions about shape distributions, precision recipes, and parallelism patterns roughly three years ahead of deployment. The TPU line’s survival across the CNN→transformer→MoE transitions is the strongest counter-evidence to the freeze risk—and the SparseCore additions show the mechanism: the family survives by absorbing signature changes into point hardware, the same absorption strategy the GPU uses, executed by an owner with perfect knowledge of its own workload portfolio.

## 6.4 Deterministic Dataflow and Tile Processors

### Primer

This family makes the most radical scheduling commitment in the chapter: it removes runtime uncertainty from the device entirely. The chip is a fabric of tiles—each combining compute units, a slice of SRAM, and a router—connected by an on-chip network with known, fixed latencies. The compiler places every operation on a tile, schedules every operand movement on the network, and emits a program in which the position of every byte on every link is known at every cycle. There are no caches to miss, no arbiters to wait on, no dynamic warp scheduler: execution time is a compile-time constant. Where the tensor processor of §6.3 is *mostly* static, this family is static by construction—the extreme point of Chapter 3’s scheduling axis.

The second commitment is that model state lives in distributed on-chip SRAM as the primary residence, not as a staging area for DRAM. Weights are placed across tile memories at compile time and stay there; activations stream between tiles along compiled routes. This is a direct assault on the §2.5 decode bound: if the weights already sit next to the arithmetic at SRAM bandwidth, the compulsory per-token weight traffic that capped the GPU at ≈48 tokens/s moves two to three orders of magnitude in bandwidth terms. The price is equally direct: on-chip SRAM capacity is minute compared to HBM, so real models shard across many chips, and the interconnect inherits the problem the memory system solved.

For a software architect the mental model is: the compiler is the operating system, the scheduler, and the memory allocator, all executed before the program runs. Everything Chapter 3 described as dynamic machinery has become a compilation problem—which is why this family lives or dies by its compiler in a way even tensor processors do not.

### The contracts, rewritten

Execution: operations fire on a compiled clock, not on readiness discovery; the execution contract is a schedule, not an instruction stream. Data placement: fully explicit, compile-time placement in distributed SRAM; the reuse-distance analysis of Chapter 2 becomes a static placement optimization. Numerical: conventional narrow formats; determinism additionally fixes reduction orders, giving bitwise-reproducible results—valuable for the §2.9 scientific and §2.11 safety signatures. Scheduling: there is none at runtime; queueing exists only at the device boundary where nondeterministic reality (arriving requests) meets the deterministic core. Communication: on-chip routes are compiled; chip-to-chip traffic in the scaled systems is also scheduled, extending determinism across the node—software-scheduled networking replacing switch arbitration [5.10]. Software: the compiler must solve placement, routing, and timing simultaneously; unsupported dynamic behavior (data-dependent shapes, dynamic control flow) must be padded to worst case, restructured, or handled at the boundary—the static-scheduling limitation of §3.7 in its purest form.

### Evidence anchors

Groq’s tensor streaming processor is the purist exemplar (class A): 220 MiB of on-chip SRAM at roughly 80 TB/s aggregate bandwidth, no caches and no arbiters, with the compiler scheduling every functional unit each cycle [5.10]. Published follow-on work documents batch-one transformer inference exploiting exactly this contract [6.6]. SambaNova’s SN40L (class A/B, vendor-published architecture papers) represents the reconfigurable-dataflow variant with a crucial difference: a three-tier memory system—on the order of half a gigabyte of on-chip SRAM, tens of gigabytes of HBM, and terabyte-scale directly attached DDR per socket—acknowledging that one tier cannot serve both residency and capacity [6.5]. Tenstorrent’s tile processors (class B, vendor disclosure) mark the programmable end: RISC-V cores in each tile, Ethernet as the scale-out fabric, and a stated bet that tile-local programmability will matter more than perfect determinism [6.15]. The three exemplars usefully span the family’s internal spectrum: maximal determinism, memory-tier pragmatism, and tile programmability.

### A worked anchor: inverting the decode bound

The §2.5 arithmetic can be rerun for this family (author-computed, same idealized assumptions). A device holding weights in 220 MiB of SRAM at 80 TB/s offers roughly 24× the weight-streaming bandwidth of the H100’s HBM—but 0.3% of its capacity. A 70 GB model therefore shards across hundreds of such chips, and the single-sequence token ceiling is set no longer by memory but by the compiled pipeline’s traversal latency across the fabric. The family thus does not remove the decode bound; it exchanges a bandwidth bound for a capacity-and-interconnect bound. Whether the exchange wins depends on the workload term of the necessity test: at high sustained request rates over a small stable model set, amortizing hundreds of chips can beat underutilized GPUs on latency and tokens per joule; at low or bursty demand, the sharded fleet is stranded capital—§2.14’s condition 4 again, now applied to a challenger rather than an incumbent.

### Envelope

Inside: latency-bounded inference at small batch over stable models—the §2.5 decode signature, plus the deterministic-response signatures of §2.11 where bitwise reproducibility and bounded timing are contract requirements rather than luxuries. Streaming pipelines with fixed shapes (§2.8 video at fixed resolution) also fit naturally. Outside: workloads whose shapes or control flow are data-dependent (MoE routing, dynamic graphs) force worst-case padding that dissolves the efficiency; workloads larger than the deployed SRAM footprint inherit sharding costs; and rapidly changing model portfolios stress the compiler exactly as they stress the tensor processor’s, but harder, because the compiler owns strictly more of the machine.

### Continuum

The family is presently a datacenter and enterprise-appliance phenomenon: the compiler-owned contract needs deployment environments with controlled model sets, and the sharded-SRAM economics need sustained request volume. The architectural ideas migrate downward selectively—compile-time scheduling and tile-local SRAM appear in NPUs (§6.5) and in automotive accelerators where determinism serves certification (§2.11)—but the full contract, including scheduled networking, has no client-scale instance; a phone has neither the model stability nor the request rate to amortize it.

### Position and risk

This family is the principal challenger in the contested middle of §5.11: low-batch decode is its chosen battlefield, and its contract change is one the GPU platform cannot copy without abandoning dynamic scheduling. Its most exposed condition is 4’s software-capital term—every deployment requires the vendor’s compiler to swallow the customer’s models whole—with condition 1 drift (dynamic, data-dependent model structure) as the architectural threat: every step the industry takes toward runtime-dependent computation (long adaptive reasoning traces, dynamic routing, tool use) enlarges exactly the behavior this family must pad for. Its durable niches, conversely, are wherever determinism is itself the requirement: reproducible science, certified real-time, and latency-guaranteed serving.

## 6.5 NPUs: Integrated Accelerators for Client, Mobile, and Embedded Systems

### Primer

An NPU is not a small datacenter accelerator. It is an SoC block—a matrix engine (usually a modest systolic array), a vector unit, DMA engines, and a slice of local SRAM—that shares the SoC’s LPDDR memory, participates in its power-domain hierarchy, and is programmed exclusively through a graph compiler. Chapter 1 established its true lineage: the NPU descends from mobile DSPs, ISPs, and sensor hubs, not from GPUs shrunk down (§1.4). Its governing constraint is the one quantified in §2.10: whole-system energy, dominated by wake scope and shared-memory traffic rather than by arithmetic. An NPU is therefore designed backward from the power domain: the question is not “how many TOPS” but “which subgraphs can execute without waking the CPU cluster, the GPU, or—in the always-on case—DRAM itself.”

The family spans three distinct design points that share a contract but not a scale. The **SoC NPU** (phones, AI PCs) executes foreground models at tens of TOPS within a shared thermal budget. The **microNPU** (tens to hundreds of GOPS) sits beside a microcontroller, executes a compiled command stream from flash or local SRAM, and exists to make small models feasible within milliwatt envelopes [1.13]. The **near-sensor engine** moves a first inference stage into or beside the sensor, so that raw data need not cross the SoC at all—the ShiDianNao principle of §1.5 in production form. A software architect should treat these as one contract at three wake scopes: each successive design point buys lower energy by narrowing what it can run.

### The contracts, rewritten

Execution and data placement follow the tensor-processor pattern in miniature—static compilation onto a matrix engine with explicit local memory—but two contracts differ in kind, not degree. The scheduling contract is shared: the NPU competes with the display controller, ISP, modem, and GPU for LPDDR bandwidth and thermal headroom, under an OS power manager that may power-gate it entirely; §3.7’s cross-layer scheduling and §4.4’s SoC-synchronization stressors are the daily reality, not edge cases. The software contract is defined by *coverage and fallback*: the runtime partitions each model graph, executes supported subgraphs on the NPU, and falls back to CPU or GPU for the remainder—and §2.10 established that fallback’s copies and wakeups can erase the NPU’s entire energy advantage. Useful coverage (fraction of end-to-end time, energy, and movement on the intended engine, §3.8) is the family’s real figure of merit; vendor TOPS is nominal width, unmeasured against any admissible batch, precision, or residency assumption, and per DI-4 should never be compared across vendors without a defined workload boundary.

### Evidence anchors

Anchors here are deliberately architectural rather than competitive. Arm’s Ethos-U55 documentation (class A) is the cleanest public microNPU contract: offline compilation to a command stream, DMA-fed execution, interrupt signaling, and explicit clock- and power-control integration [1.13]. Apple’s M4-generation neural engine (class A, vendor claim) illustrates SoC-NPU scale—tens of TOPS at INT8 inside a passively cooled client budget [6.14]—while the always-on end is anchored by the sub-300 µW wake-word silicon already cited in §2.10 [2.21]. The four-orders-of-magnitude span between those last two numbers, inside one product category, is the family’s defining fact: “NPU” names a wake-scope spectrum, not a performance class. Chapter 8 treats the competitive landscape (Apple, Qualcomm, Intel, AMD, Google) in product terms.

### Envelope

Inside: quantized inference over a curated model set—vision, speech, translation, image processing, increasingly compact language models—at batch one, where the §2.10 analysis showed a narrow low-overhead engine beating a wide GPU on energy per inference; always-on sensing, where the power-domain boundary is the entire game; and sustained camera pipelines, where NPU offload protects the thermal budget that CPU/GPU execution would exhaust (§4.7). Outside: models exceeding local memory or bandwidth (the distilled-model constraint of the running example’s on-device deployment); operator sets ahead of compiler coverage—the fastest-moving failure mode, since model innovation outpaces NPU compiler releases; and any workload needing the programmability escape hatch, which by construction costs a wake. The envelope is thus defined less by arithmetic than by three budgets: local memory, operator coverage, and wake scope.

### Continuum

This family is the continuum’s low end made systematic, and its ideas flow upward as §2.16 predicts: power-domain isolation, quantized execution, and graph-partition compilation inform datacenter energy proportionality and enterprise consolidation. Automotive NPUs are the family’s certified variant—same contract, plus the determinism and isolation obligations of §2.11, which push them toward the static extremes of §6.4. The AI-PC NPU is the family’s newest design point and its most contested: it shares silicon with capable CPUs and GPUs, so its necessity case rests entirely on energy-per-inference and sustained-thermal arguments, not capability.

### Position and risk

In the §5.11 partition the NPU family holds the most secure ground in this chapter: wake-scope energy and per-unit cost floors are structural non-absorbables, and no datacenter-class contract reaches them. The necessity test passes wholesale at mobile volume—silicon area amortized across hundreds of millions of units against recurring battery-energy savings—which is why every major SoC vendor has converged on the family (class A across the industry). The exposed condition is 3, at the small end: if fallback fragments the graph badly enough, the hardware lever stops changing the system bound, and the NPU becomes idle silicon; coverage, not capability, is where NPU deployments fail. The family’s trajectory—rising operator counts, larger local SRAM, growing language-model support—is best read as a race between compiler coverage and model churn.

## 6.6 FPGAs and Reconfigurable Computing

### Primer

An FPGA is the one family whose datapath does not exist until software creates it. The fabric provides lookup tables, hardened arithmetic (DSP) blocks, distributed block RAM, and a programmable routing network; a design is a circuit description that configures all of them into a custom pipeline. The architectural consequence: an FPGA can implement *any* of this chapter’s other families—at a density, clock, and energy penalty. Classic measurements place the fabric’s cost against standard-cell ASICs at roughly 20–35× area, 3–4× clock, and an order of magnitude in dynamic power, narrowing substantially where hardened blocks carry the work [6.4]. The family’s reason to exist is therefore never peak efficiency; it is *structural flexibility after manufacturing*—the ability to build a tailored dataflow, a nonstandard numeric format, a deterministic pipeline, or a wire-speed interface engine, and to rebuild it when requirements change.

Two modern developments define the family’s AI posture. First, vendors have hardened increasingly large blocks—AI-oriented DSP variants and full spatial-vector engine arrays beside the fabric [6.16]—moving the family toward a hybrid: a coarse-grained accelerator whose glue, control, and interfaces remain soft. Second, the *overlay* pattern decouples deployment from hardware compilation: a soft processor or soft NPU is synthesized once, and models are compiled to it in seconds rather than through hours-long placement and routing. Microsoft’s Brainwave is the canonical production overlay—a synthesized “soft NPU” with model weights pinned in distributed on-chip block RAM, delivering batch-one, millisecond-scale DNN serving at datacenter scale on fleet FPGAs [6.2].

### The contracts, rewritten

Every contract becomes designable, which is the family’s signature and its burden. Execution: whatever the design implements—systolic, dataflow, pipelined—at clocks well below ASIC equivalents. Data placement: distributed block RAM gives enormous aggregate on-chip bandwidth at small capacity, naturally suiting weight-pinned streaming designs. Numerical: arbitrary—including the nonstandard narrow floating-point formats Brainwave exploited [6.2]—making the family a proving ground for precision innovations later hardened elsewhere. Scheduling and communication: as deterministic as the designer chooses; fixed pipelines with cycle-accurate timing are natural, which is why the family persists in real-time and interface-attached roles. Software: the deep problem. Hardware compilation (synthesis, placement, routing) takes hours and requires hardware skills; overlays and high-level synthesis trade away efficiency to escape that cost; and every design carries verification obligations application software never sees. The specialization budget of §4.6 is spent here on *tools and people* more than on silicon.

### Evidence anchors

Microsoft’s Catapult (class A, peer-reviewed production pilot) established fleet-scale FPGA deployment: a 1,632-server pilot accelerating Bing ranking, reporting on the order of a 95% throughput improvement per server for roughly a 10% power increase, with the fabric doubling as a network-attached processing layer [6.3]. Brainwave (class A) is the strongest published evidence that reconfigurable fabric can win a latency-bound AI serving role at scale: weights resident in on-chip RAM across the fleet, batch-one service, tens of teraflops effective per device in custom narrow formats [6.2]. The hardened-engine trajectory [6.16] is class A/B vendor disclosure. Together they bracket the family’s viable AI roles: infrastructure offload, latency-bound serving with pinned models, and hybrid fabric-plus-engines platforms.

### Envelope

Inside: workloads where one of three properties dominates—(1) interface adjacency: the computation sits on a network, storage, or sensor path and benefits from wire-speed processing without host round trips (§2.11 sensor pipelines, smart NICs, instrument and medical platforms); (2) pinned-model latency serving: models small enough for block RAM, served at batch one under tight tails [6.2]; (3) format or algorithm novelty ahead of ASIC cycles. Outside: dense large-model training and bulk serving, where the fabric’s area and clock penalties against GPUs and tensor processors are unaffordable; any deployment lacking hardware-design capability; and models too large for on-chip pinning, which force the DRAM traffic patterns the family handles no better than cheaper alternatives.

### Continuum

The family inverts the usual continuum pattern: it is strongest at the two ends and weakest in the middle. In hyperscale infrastructure it serves as fleet-programmable offload where requirements change faster than ASIC cycles [6.3]. In embedded, automotive, industrial, and instrument systems—the family’s historical home—it provides deterministic pipelines, custom interfaces, and twenty-year lifecycles that no volume-driven SoC will serve. The middle (client, general enterprise serving) belongs to cheaper fixed-function competitors precisely because those deployments have stable requirements and no interface adjacency.

### Position and risk

In the §5.11 partition, the family holds secure ground where flexibility-after-manufacturing is itself the requirement—long-lifecycle embedded, interface-attached processing, format experimentation—territory no fixed design can occupy by definition. In the contested middle it competes only transiently: each time a workload stabilizes, its FPGA implementation becomes an ASIC or NPU business case, and the fabric moves on—serving, in effect, as the industry’s staging tier for the necessity test: workloads that pass condition 1 through 3 often prove condition 4 on an FPGA before committing to silicon. The standing risk is the software condition: high-level toolchains have promised software-engineer accessibility for two decades against limited adoption, and every AI role the family wins must be won despite that barrier.

## 6.7 Wafer-Scale Computing

### Primer

Every other family in this chapter accepts the reticle limit—the maximum die a lithography stepper can expose, roughly 800 mm²—and buys scale by connecting many dies through packages and networks. Wafer-scale integration refuses the premise: it fabricates one logical device across an entire wafer, leaving the inter-reticle scribe lines wired so that what would have been chip-to-chip communication becomes on-silicon routing. The architectural consequence is a step change in two quantities at once: on-chip memory capacity and the bandwidth between compute and that memory. Cerebras’s third-generation wafer-scale engine (class A/B, vendor disclosure) integrates on the order of 900,000 cores, 44 GB of SRAM distributed among them, and roughly 21 PB/s of aggregate memory bandwidth on a 46,225 mm² device of four trillion transistors [6.7]—per-wafer figures, to be compared against packages only with that boundary stated.

The design is best understood as the §6.4 tile fabric taken to its physical limit: small cores, each with local SRAM, on a 2D mesh, with dataflow scheduling—but with the mesh so large that a mid-sized model’s weights and activations fit *entirely on the device*. For models beyond on-wafer capacity, the production systems stream weights from external memory appliances, inverting the usual residency: activations stay, weights flow. Wafer-scale also forces engineering no other family faces: yield is survived rather than avoided (defective cores are mapped out and routed around by construction), and power delivery and cooling for a whole wafer (tens of kilowatts) require purpose-built packaging, making the deployable unit an appliance, never a card.

### The contracts, rewritten

Data placement: the defining change—model-scale state at SRAM distance, eliminating the off-chip weight traffic that dominates every DRAM-fed family; Chapter 2’s memory-distance figure collapses two tiers. Communication: chip-to-chip links become mesh hops of known geometry; the §4.4 collective stressors shrink at pod scale because a single wafer replaces racks of discrete accelerators—until the model outgrows the wafer and reintroduces them at appliance granularity. Execution and scheduling: dataflow over a huge tile array, compiler-placed; sparsity is handled natively by skipping zero operands in the fine-grained cores, giving the family an unusual affinity for the §2.7 sparse signatures. Software: a single-vendor stack compiling whole models to one enormous device—simpler in one respect (no intra-model distributed programming for models that fit) and riskier in another (one vendor, one toolchain, no ecosystem redundancy). Numerical and reliability: conventional formats; fault tolerance is designed-in redundancy at fabrication, with the appliance as the failure domain—the §4.8 blast-radius question at its largest.

### Envelope

Inside: training and scientific workloads whose working sets fit on-wafer, where the elimination of inter-device communication converts §4.3’s pipeline-bubble and collective stressors into on-mesh traffic—including irregular and sparse scientific AI (§2.9), a niche the family has actively cultivated; and single-appliance deployments where one integrated device replaces a cluster’s integration burden—an *enterprise* proposition as much as a hyperscale one, since a small organization can operate an appliance more easily than a distributed GPU pod. Outside: models and context far beyond on-wafer state, where weight streaming rebuilds the memory hierarchy the family exists to delete; elastic multi-tenant serving, where the appliance granularity resists fractional allocation; and any deployment below the appliance's price and power class—there is, by construction, no client or embedded instance of this family. Wafer-scale is the chapter's one family with no downward migration path on the continuum; its ideas travel (tile meshes, distributed SRAM, defect-tolerant routing inform §6.4 and §6.5 designs), but its physical form does not.

### Position and risk

In the §5.11 partition, wafer-scale is a contested-middle occupant with one secure architectural claim: it is the only family that changes the *scale* term of the memory contract—capacity at SRAM distance—by orders of magnitude rather than increments, a change the GPU platform cannot absorb within reticle-limited packaging. The necessity-test exposure is condition 4 in its purest form: the appliance model must win against clusters of commodity accelerators whose software, resale, and operational ecosystems are vastly larger; the §2.14 Graphcore precedent shows how that term punishes even sound architecture. The family’s durable advantage grows with exactly one industry trend—models and scientific workloads whose *communication* costs, not arithmetic, dominate cluster economics—and shrinks with every improvement in commodity scale-up fabrics.

## 6.8 Memory-Centric Architectures and Processing-in-Memory

### Primer

Every family so far moves data toward compute. This family moves compute toward data—placing arithmetic inside or beside the memory arrays themselves—because for the workloads of §2.5 and §2.6 the dominant cost is not computing on bytes but *transporting* them across the memory interface. The physics is the §4.2 energy hierarchy: an off-chip DRAM access costs two to three orders of magnitude more than the arithmetic it feeds [4.1], and the interface pins that bound §2.5’s decode arithmetic are a package-level chokepoint. Putting even modest compute on the memory side of that chokepoint changes what must cross it: results instead of operands.

Three architecturally distinct flavors share the label. **PIM in commodity DRAM** embeds narrow SIMD units beside the banks of otherwise-standard HBM or LPDDR: Samsung’s Aquabolt-XL places a 16-lane FP16 unit per bank pair inside HBM2 stacks, exploiting bank-level parallelism to present roughly 4× the effective internal bandwidth of the external interface, with vendor-reported system gains of ~2× performance and ~70% energy reduction on bandwidth-bound kernels (class B/C) [6.8]; the same line extends to LPDDR5-PIM for *mobile* devices, where the interface energy it avoids is battery energy—making this the family’s most direct client-and-embedded instance. UPMEM’s DPUs embed general-purpose cores per bank in DDR4 DIMMs (class A, niche) [6.9]. **Near-memory all-SRAM inference** eliminates the off-chip tier entirely: IBM’s NorthPole (class C, peer-reviewed)[^northpole] distributes 224 MB of SRAM among 256 cores so that weights and activations never leave the die during inference, reporting ~22× the frame rate and ~25× less energy than a same-process-node GPU on ResNet-50-class workloads—a deliberately matched-node comparison whose boundary (12 nm, vision-scale models that fit on-chip) must accompany every citation of it [6.10]. **Memory-tier composition** attaches large cheap capacity directly to an accelerator so that tiering, not a single technology, serves both residency and capacity—the SN40L’s terabyte-class DDR tier for held-resident expert weights being the shipping example [6.5], and the general direction most likely to reach mainstream serving first.

For a software architect the essential novelty is that the memory system stops being a passive hierarchy and becomes an execution resource with its own programming model: operations must be *placed* into memory, coherence between host and in-memory compute must be managed explicitly, and data layout across banks becomes a first-order correctness-and-performance decision rather than a tuning detail.

### The contracts, rewritten

Data placement inverts: instead of staging data near compute, the compiler stages compute near data, and bank-level layout becomes the analogue of §3.4’s dataflow mapping. Execution fragments: host code, accelerator kernels, and in-memory operations form a three-level execution model with no shared instruction set. Communication: the external memory interface carries reduced, result-level traffic; internal bank-level bandwidth—unavailable to any external device—is the resource being harvested. Numerical: in-DRAM units are narrow (FP16/INT) by area necessity, constraining which operators can move inward. Scheduling and software: the deep problem, sharper here than anywhere except §6.6—there is no portable programming model for in-memory compute; each design exposes proprietary primitives, host runtimes must orchestrate placement and consistency, and the JEDEC-standard economics of commodity memory resist nonstandard silicon in the stack. The family’s architecture is largely solved; its software contract is not.

### Envelope

Inside: precisely the signatures Chapter 2 marked as bandwidth-bound with low arithmetic intensity—embedding gather and sparse lookup (§2.6), where in-memory reduction turns scattered reads into local operations; low-batch weight streaming (§2.5), where in-memory matrix-vector work attacks the decode bound at its source; and mobile always-on paths, where LPDDR-PIM shrinks the wake scope §2.10 identified as the true energy boundary. Outside: high-arithmetic-intensity dense work (the compute-rich families win there by construction); any workload needing operators beyond the narrow in-memory set; and—for the all-SRAM flavor—models beyond on-die capacity, the same cliff as §6.4’s. The envelope is the mirror image of the tensor processor’s: this family is strongest exactly where §6.3 is weakest.

### Continuum

Uniquely broad in principle, thin in practice. Datacenter: HBM-PIM prototypes and tiered-memory accelerators target serving economics. Enterprise: tiering (the third flavor) suits private deployments whose capacity needs exceed HBM budgets. Client and mobile: LPDDR-PIM is architecturally the best-matched instance of the family—the interface it shortens is the one battery energy pays for—and its adoption is gated not by architecture but by the commodity-memory supply chain’s tolerance for differentiated parts. Embedded: near-sensor SRAM inference (§6.5’s near-sensor engines are this family’s smallest instances, seen from the memory side). The continuum coverage is therefore real but hostage to standardization.

### Position and risk

The §5.11 partition placed near-memory computation in the contested middle, and this section sharpens why: the bounds it attacks are real and measured, but every variant faces a non-architectural gate. In-DRAM PIM must pass through memory-standards economics; all-SRAM inference must live within on-die capacity; tier composition—the least radical—inherits ordinary ecosystem competition. The necessity test’s condition 3 is unusually strong here (the lever genuinely changes the bound rather than relocating it), while condition 4’s software-enablement term is the binding obstacle: without a portable programming model, each deployment is a bespoke integration. The family’s trajectory is best read as a race between standardization efforts and the GPU platform’s own memory-side absorption (larger caches, HBM evolution, NVLink-attached capacity tiers).

## 6.9 Neuromorphic Computing: A Forward-Looking Overview

### Primer

Neuromorphic architectures abandon the clocked, dense, batch-oriented execution model shared by everything above. Computation is *event-driven*: silicon neurons integrate incoming spikes and emit spikes only when thresholds cross; synaptic state lives beside the neurons in distributed SRAM; and an asynchronous mesh routes spike packets only when spikes exist. Energy is therefore proportional to *activity*, not to provisioned width—when nothing changes at the input, almost nothing switches on the chip. This is the logical endpoint of two threads that run through this whole survey: co-located memory and compute (taken further than §6.8), and computation gated by data sparsity (taken further than any sparse-matrix scheme—sparsity here is temporal, in the signal itself).

The family’s production evidence remains research-class. IBM’s TrueNorth demonstrated a million-neuron chip at tens of milliwatts (class C) [6.11]; Intel’s Loihi generations added programmable neuron models, on-chip learning rules, and roughly million-neuron scale on a modern node, with an open software framework [6.12][6.13]. These systems validate the energy model without yet validating a mainstream workload: the dominant AI stack—dense tensors, backpropagation, transformer serving—maps poorly onto spiking substrates, and converting trained networks to spikes generally costs accuracy or latency. The family’s near-term relevance is therefore not the datacenter but the *extreme edge*: always-on sensory processing (audio, vision, tactile, biosignal), robotic reflex loops, and event-camera pipelines, where inputs are naturally sparse in time and the §2.10 wake-scope logic applies at microwatt scale—precisely the territory where §6.5’s always-on engines already live, approached from a different theoretical foundation. NorthPole, sometimes grouped with this family through institutional lineage, is better classified in §6.8: it is synchronous near-memory inference, not spiking—an example of why the Charter’s taxonomy discipline matters.

### Contracts and position

Every contract changes at once—execution (event-driven), placement (synaptic co-location), numerical (spikes, graded or binary), scheduling (there is none; the input schedules the machine), communication (address-event routing), software (no mainstream framework lowers to spikes)—which is exactly the family’s adoption problem: it offers no incremental path from the existing stack. In §5.11 terms it holds a structurally secure claim on activity-proportional energy at the always-on edge and a speculative claim everywhere else. Against the necessity test it currently fails condition 1 for mainstream workloads—no demonstrated residual bound that spiking uniquely relieves—while plausibly passing all four conditions for micro-power sensory niches. Evidence class E dominates any forward projection; per the Charter, this section makes none beyond noting that the family’s fortunes track the emergence of workloads that are natively event-based, a category robotics and wearables may yet supply.

## 6.11 Engineering Takeaways

- Every viable accelerator family is a bundle of contract changes—not a faster device—and the two organizing axes are when uncertainty is resolved (runtime → compile time → design time → input-driven) and where state resides relative to arithmetic (DRAM → distributed SRAM → in-memory), so a family is evaluated by locating it on those axes and asking which Chapter 2 signatures live there.
- The systolic mechanism—reuse from geometry—has been absorbed by every side including the GPU, which means committing a whole die to one large array is a commitment to shape stability, not to the mechanism itself.
- Tensor processors and deterministic dataflow machines are both compiler-owned designs, but they divide the contested middle: the former wins dense portfolios at owner-scale volume, the latter bids for latency-bounded decode and reproducible execution, and both carry software-capital exposure as their dominant necessity-test risk.
- NPUs hold the most secure beyond-GPU territory because wake-scope energy and per-unit cost floors are structural non-absorbables; their real figure of merit is useful coverage—the fraction of end-to-end time and energy that stays on the engine—so fallback behavior, not TOPS, decides deployments.
- FPGAs and wafer-scale systems win where their unique property is itself the requirement—flexibility after manufacturing and model-scale state at SRAM distance respectively—and serve elsewhere as the industry’s staging tier and communication-bound specialist rather than as general competitors.
- Memory-centric designs attack the survey’s most-measured bounds (decode streaming, embedding gather, mobile wake energy) with the strongest condition-3 case in the chapter, yet remain gated by a non-architectural condition: no portable programming model and commodity-memory standards economics.
- Across all families the recurring verdict of §2.14 applies: architecture selects the candidate, but software capital, workload stability, and volume decide the outcome—so the product landscapes of Chapters 7 and 8 should be read as evidence about those conditions, never as rankings of architectural merit.
