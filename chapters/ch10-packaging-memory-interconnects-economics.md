# Chapter 10. Packaging, Memory, Interconnects, and Economics

**Snapshot date: mid-2026** for the technology-status facts below, with the Chapter 7 stability caveat. This chapter descends beneath the architecture families to the physical and economic substrate they share: the memory technologies that set every bandwidth bound in this survey, the packaging that determines what can sit next to what, the fabrics that make pods out of packages, and the power, cooling, manufacturing, and cost structures that increasingly bind the whole system. The organizing claim is continuous with the rest of the survey: the competitive frontier has moved from logic to *integration*—the package is the new node, the rack is the new server, and the facility is the new rack.

## 10.1 Memory Technologies

### HBM: the bound made of silicon

Every worked bound in this survey—§2.5's decode arithmetic, Figure 6.8's landscape, Figure 7.1's convergence—terminates in high-bandwidth memory. HBM's architecture (stacked DRAM dies, through-silicon vias, a wide interface crossing an interposer) is what makes multi-TB/s packages possible, and its generational cadence now paces accelerator roadmaps as much as logic does: HBM4 reached mass production across the 2025–2026 window, with extended variants following and the MI400-class parts built around it [10.1][7.3]. Three structural facts matter more than any speed grade. **Supply is concentrated** in three vendors, and HBM allocation has repeatedly been the practical limiter on accelerator shipments—memory supply is a condition-4 term (§2.14) that architecture cannot design around. **Cost share is inverted**: on flagship accelerators the HBM stack plausibly rivals or exceeds the logic die in bill-of-materials share (class D industry analysis), which reframes "memory-centric architecture" (§6.8) as an economic observation, not only a technical one. **Capacity and bandwidth scale differently**: stacking adds capacity faster than interface width adds bandwidth, which is why the capacity-per-bandwidth ratios in Figure 7.1 cluster, and why architectures that escape the interface entirely (§6.4, §6.7, §6.8) remain permanently interesting.

### LPDDR, DDR, and the tiers

LPDDR carries the entire client half of the survey: §8.1's on-package capacity race and §2.10's energy analysis are LPDDR stories, since its low-power interface is what makes shared-memory SoCs viable inside battery and thermal envelopes. Its constraint triangle—capacity, bandwidth, power—cannot be optimized simultaneously, which is why client decode remains bandwidth-bound (§8.1) even as capacities reach 128 GB. Commodity DDR reappears at the opposite scale as the *capacity tier* of §6.8's third flavor (SN40L's terabyte tier [6.5]); CXL-attached memory generalizes the same idea over a standard fabric, with mid-2026 deployment concentrated in memory pooling rather than AI-critical paths (class B/D). The unified picture is the §3.5 memory contract priced out: every tier trades bandwidth for capacity for cost, and system architecture is the art of matching each Chapter 2 signature to the cheapest tier that satisfies it.

## 10.2 Packaging: The Reticle Limit and Its Workarounds

The reticle limit (~800 mm²) is the fixed physical fact from which modern accelerator diversity radiates: Chapter 6's wafer-scale family refuses it (§6.7); everyone else engineers around it. **2.5D integration**—logic and HBM on silicon interposers (CoWoS-class)—is the mainstream workaround and the industry's most persistent chokepoint: advanced-packaging capacity, not wafer starts, has repeatedly gated accelerator supply (class D). **Chiplet decomposition** splits what one die cannot hold: dual-die flagship GPUs presented as one logical device, 3D-stacked compute-on-base designs (MI300 lineage), and dual-die dataflow sockets [6.5] are all shipping instances, making the §3.9 on-package communication contract a first-order design surface. **3D hybrid bonding** extends the direction—die-on-die at bond densities that begin to blur the memory/logic boundary, with cache-stacked CPUs shipping and logic-on-logic accelerators disclosed (class B/C). Standards are consolidating the seam: UCIe for die-to-die links, and the UALink consortium's 2026 chiplet specification extending scale-up protocols into the package [10.2]—early evidence for a future in which accelerator sockets are *composed* from vendor-mixed chiplets, with obvious consequences for the condition-4 barriers of Chapters 7 and 9.

The architectural summary: packaging has become the axis along which the memory-distance hierarchy of Figure 2.3 is physically renegotiated. Each packaging generation moves some tier closer—HBM across an interposer, SRAM onto the die stack, and (prospectively) optics onto the package—which is why this chapter sits between the family chapters and the futures chapter: most of Chapter 11's credible futures are packaging stories.

## 10.3 Fabrics: Scale-Up, Scale-Out, and the Standards Response

Chapter 7 recorded the fork: proprietary scale-up fabrics (NVLink domains, TPU's optically switched tori, Trainium's NeuronSwitch) versus commodity Ethernet (Maia 200, Tenstorrent). The mid-2026 standards landscape is the industry's attempt to give the second path the properties of the first. **UALink**—1.0 ratified in 2025, with 2026 additions covering in-network compute, chiplet integration, and manageability [10.2]—defines an open memory-semantics scale-up fabric for accelerator pods: the §4.4 collective and coherence machinery, standardized. **Ultra Ethernet 1.0** [10.3] retrofits scale-out Ethernet with the transport features distributed training exposed as missing (multipathing, modern congestion control, in-network collective support). Together they encode a strategic bet visible throughout Chapters 7–8: that the fabric layer, like the kernel layer in Chapter 9, can be commoditized—and the same caveat applies, since standards specify semantics, not the tuned collective libraries where §9.2 located the moat.

The physical frontier is optical. Co-packaged optics moved from roadmap to product in 2026: switch silicon with optical engines bonded directly to the ASIC, vendor-claimed power reductions around 3.5× versus pluggable optics at hundreds of Tb/s per switch, using foundry photonics processes with 3D-bonded electronic-photonic stacks [10.4]. The survey's framing: CPO attacks §4.4's power ceiling (network energy as a growing share of pod power) rather than a latency bound, and its 2026 beachhead—switch-level, not accelerator-level—establishes the supply chain that Chapter 11's more radical photonic proposals would require. Optics reaching the *package* (accelerator-to-accelerator, then accelerator-to-memory) is the watch item that would re-draw Figure 2.3's distance hierarchy outright.

## 10.4 Power, Cooling, and the Facility as a System Component

The power trajectory documented across Chapters 7–8—1.0–1.4 kW packages, 72-accelerator racks, liquid cooling as a baseline requirement—has made §4.7's analysis literal: the binding purchase constraint for many buyers is now megawatts and cooling capacity, not budget or silicon supply (class D, consistent industry reporting). Three consequences organize the topic. **Liquid cooling is an architecture decision**, not a facility detail: direct-to-chip loops determine rack density, which determines fabric reach, which determines pod topology—the §4.4 chain running backward from plumbing. **Energy proportionality has become fleet economics**: the idle and low-load behavior Chapter 1 flagged [1.4] scales to inference fleets whose diurnal load makes low-utilization efficiency a first-order cost term—one the mobile-derived techniques of §2.16 directly address. **The grid is entering the system boundary**: facility power procurement, siting, and multi-year lead times now appear in accelerator-strategy discussions (class D), which the survey notes as a boundary condition and leaves to its economic appendix: for hyperscale planning, joules are increasingly the scarce currency in which Chapter 2's bounds are priced.

## 10.5 Manufacturing and Supply as Architectural Constraints

Four concentrations shape what can actually be built, and each has appeared implicitly earlier in the survey. Leading-edge logic is effectively single-sourced at the frontier node; advanced packaging capacity gates accelerator assembly; HBM supply is three vendors deep; and export-control regimes partition the global market into distinct product tiers (stated here as a structural fact of the 2026 landscape, without policy analysis). For the survey's framework these are condition-4 terms of a kind the early chapters under-weighted and Chapter 8's automotive section surfaced first (supply sovereignty as a non-financial necessity-test term, §8.5): a design that cannot secure wafer starts, CoWoS slots, or HBM allocation fails economically regardless of merit, and hyperscaler internal-silicon programs (§7.2) are partly *supply insurance*—vertical integration purchasing allocation certainty, not only efficiency.

## 10.6 The Cost Stack, Assembled

The chapter's threads compose into the survey's full cost stack for a deployed unit of AI work, layered from silicon to service: die cost (node, yield, reticle economics) → memory (HBM/LPDDR share) → packaging (interposer, assembly, test) → board and node integration → fabric (switches, optics, cabling) → rack and cooling → facility power and space → software enablement (Chapter 9's capital) → operations, reliability margin, and utilization loss (Chapter 4's headroom) → risk terms (supply, obsolescence, stranded software). Every architecture family of Chapter 6 redistributes weight across this stack rather than shrinking it uniformly—wafer-scale moves fabric cost into yield engineering; PIM moves logic cost into memory; NPUs move it into per-unit area—and the §2.14 test is, in cost-stack terms, the question of whether a redistribution lowers the total for a given workload portfolio at a given volume. Appendix G operationalizes exactly this arithmetic for decision-makers; Chapter 12 draws the conclusions.

## 10.7 Engineering Takeaways

- Memory technology paces the industry: HBM generations, supply allocation, and cost share now constrain accelerator roadmaps as strongly as logic scaling, which elevates memory sourcing from procurement detail to architecture strategy.
- Packaging is the axis on which the memory-distance hierarchy is renegotiated—interposers, chiplets, and hybrid bonding each move a tier closer to arithmetic—so packaging capacity and standards (UCIe, UALink chiplets) belong in any long-horizon hardware assessment.
- The fabric layer is bifurcating into proprietary scale-up versus standardized Ethernet-lineage alternatives (UALink, Ultra Ethernet), a live experiment in whether interconnect can be commoditized the way kernels were; collective-library capital, not link specs, will likely decide it.
- Co-packaged optics is photonics' commercial beachhead, attacking network power rather than latency; optics reaching the accelerator package is the development that would restructure the survey's distance hierarchy and should be tracked as a leading indicator.
- Power and cooling have become binding constraints ahead of capital for many deployments, making energy the currency in which architectural bounds are priced, liquid-cooling capability an architectural parameter, and energy proportionality a fleet-economics requirement.
- Manufacturing and supply concentration are condition-4 terms: allocation certainty—wafer starts, packaging slots, HBM—now rivals efficiency among the rational motives for vertical integration, and any necessity-test evaluation at scale must include it.
