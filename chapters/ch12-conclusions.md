# Chapter 12. Conclusions

A survey earns its conclusions only if they were purchased earlier in the text. This chapter therefore does not introduce new claims; it assembles the ones the preceding chapters established, states where each was demonstrated and what evidence carries it, and converts the assembly into guidance. The structure moves from findings (§12.1) through their confirmation and complication by the observed landscape (§12.2–§12.3), to the continuum synthesis (§12.4), to role-specific guidance (§12.5–§12.7), and finally to what this survey could not resolve (§12.8) and how it should be revised (§12.9).

## 12.1 The Findings, Assembled

Seven findings constitute the survey's result. Each is stated with its strongest single piece of evidence and the chapter that established it.

**F1 — The two transitions are real, and software succeeded in both.** CPU optimization did not fail; it exposed limits in parallelism, locality, bandwidth, and energy that software alone could not move economically (Chapter 1), and GPU optimization is now repeating the pattern against a different set of bounds (Chapters 2, 5). The strongest evidence is quantitative: measured software gains—15% and 3× from IO-aware attention, 2–4× from KV paging (§2.5)—coexisting with hard residual bounds those same techniques cannot touch, such as the ≈48 tokens/s batch-one ceiling computed from public datasheets (§2.5). The transitions are not stories about software inadequacy; they are stories about software optimization *converging on physics*.

**F2 — Hardware necessity is conditional, decidable, and frequently negative.** The four-condition test of §2.1—residual bound, material consequence, effective lever, lifetime economics—was executed on real systems in §2.14 and returned all three possible verdicts: specialize (TPU v1, with published 15–30× throughput and 30–80× perf/W under production latency bounds), market-no (Nervana, Graphcore), and stay-on-GPU (enterprise private serving). The test's inputs are measurable in advance. This finding is the survey's methodological core: "is specialization justified?" is not a matter of opinion or vendor claim but of arithmetic over workload signatures, deployment objectives, and lifecycle economics.

**F3 — Condition 4 dominates, and software capital is its largest term.** Across every case this survey examined—historical (§2.14), family-level (Chapter 6's position-and-risk sections), and landscape-level (§7.3's consolidations)—no outcome was decided by silicon merit alone, and most were decided by software enablement, workload stability, volume, and distribution. Chapter 9 located the mechanism precisely: the moat sits in distributed libraries, serving policy, numerical recipes, and operational tooling—layers a challenger cannot mirror by porting a kernel language. The 2025–2026 record supplied the sharpest confirmation available: an incumbent paying billions to license a challenger's architecture (§7.3) is condition 3 vindicated and condition 4 enforced, in a single transaction.

**F4 — The GPU's efficiency envelope is real, bounded, and partially absorbable.** The envelope—parallelism, coalescing, lane regularity, occupancy, batchability, amortized overhead (§2.13, §5.9)—defines where GPUs are unbeatable and where they leak. Section 5.11 partitioned the leaks: bounds the platform has absorbed (matrix density, narrow precision, movement overhead, scale-up communication—Table 5.6, each with shipped evidence), bounds structurally outside any throughput contract (wake-scope energy, provable worst-case timing, near-sensor locality, embedded cost floors), and a contested middle (low-batch decode, routing, near-memory lookup). Every subsequent chapter validated the partition: the secure bounds are exactly where NPUs and embedded silicon thrive uncontested (Chapters 6, 8), and the contested middle is exactly where the commercial battles occurred (Chapter 7).

**F5 — The second transition produced families, not a successor—and the families are contract bundles.** Chapter 6 showed that every viable beyond-GPU architecture changes at least one contract the GPU cannot adopt without ceasing to be itself: compiler-owned time (§6.4), model-scale SRAM (§6.7), computation at the memory (§6.8), power-domain-first design (§6.5), post-manufacturing structure (§6.6), input-driven execution (§6.9). Two axes organize all of them—when uncertainty is resolved, and where state lives relative to arithmetic (Figure 6.1)—and no point on those axes dominates the others, because each purchase of efficiency narrows an envelope. This is why the pluralism is permanent: the diversity of workload signatures (Chapter 2) maps onto a diversity of rational contract choices (Chapters 3–4), and no amount of engineering collapses that mapping to a point.

**F6 — The binding constraints have moved to memory, communication, power, and supply.** The survey's worked bounds are memory bounds (§2.5, §2.6); the 2026 flagship competition is a memory-capacity race (Figure 7.1); the fabric fork and standards response are communication economics (§7.2, §10.3); facility power now gates deployments ahead of capital (§10.4); and allocation of HBM and packaging capacity has become a strategic term (§10.5). Arithmetic peak—the historically advertised quantity—no longer separates serious contenders anywhere in the landscape.

**F7 — The continuum is one design space with many binding constraints, and architecture flows across it in both directions.** The same families recur from microwatt always-on silicon to megawatt pods, resized against different binding constraints (Figure 6.10); unified memory and power discipline migrated upward while quantization and compilation migrated downward (§8.6); and the deployment classes reached *different rational verdicts on the same workload* (§2.14's hyperscale-yes, enterprise-no). Treating datacenter and edge as separate industries misreads both.

## 12.2 What the Observed Landscape Confirms

A framework assembled in Chapters 1–6 was, in effect, tested out-of-sample by the mid-2026 landscape of Chapters 7–8, and three predictions held in detail. The second transition became industrial fact precisely where the framework said condition 4 was satisfiable—every major cloud now fields internal tensor processors at generation depth (§7.2), while merchant challengers consolidated into incumbents, anchors, and acquisitions (§7.3). Inference-first silicon emerged exactly as the workload analysis implied once serving economics dominate fleet cost (§2.5, §7.2, and now test-time scaling deepening the trend, §11.7). And the client landscape organized itself around the wake-scope and coverage logic of §2.10/§6.5—down to vendors shipping curated OS-resident models to escape the coverage race (§9.5). The framework also survived its hardest test: events it did not specifically predict (the licensing-in of an entire challenger contract, supply allocation as a motive for vertical integration) were expressible within it without modification—as extensions of the absorption partition and of condition 4 respectively—which is the practical meaning of a taxonomy that generalizes.

## 12.3 Why Specialization Continues—and Why It Stays Plural

The forward-looking core of the survey compresses into one paragraph of physics and one of economics. The physics: data movement costs orders of magnitude more than arithmetic at every measured node (§4.2), wake scope spans four orders of magnitude of energy (§2.10), worst-case timing cannot be proven through dynamically shared machinery (§4.5), and distance to the sensor is not negotiable (§2.11). These gradients do not expire with any process node, so the pressure to specialize—to move computation toward state, toward the sensor, toward compile-time certainty, toward smaller wake scopes—renews with every workload generation. The economics: specialization pays only where its conditions hold, the conditions vary by deployment class and workload stability, and flexibility retains option value everywhere models keep changing (§4.9). Physics guarantees the pressure; economics fragments the response. Together they yield the survey's standing forecast: a permanent, moving equilibrium between a broad platform that absorbs what it can and a ring of contract-changing families holding what it cannot—with the boundary between them redrawn each generation by exactly the absorption dynamics of §5.11, and with Chapter 11's futures entering (when they do) through the incumbent's packaging rather than around it.

## 12.4 Convergence and Divergence Across the Continuum

The continuum synthesis inverts a common intuition. The *ideas* converge: locality, explicit movement, quantization, right-sized parallelism, power gating, and graph compilation now appear at every scale (§2.16, §8.6), and the family taxonomy is shared end to end. The *implementations* diverge, permanently, because each deployment class prices the same stressors differently (Table 4.2)—and the divergence is itself the strongest practical argument for this survey's method: an architect who learns the contracts and stressors once can read any point on the continuum, while an architect who learns products must relearn each segment separately. The running example closed the loop (§6.10): one document assistant, five deployment classes, five different rational hardware answers, one analytical framework.

## 12.5 Guidance for Software Architects

The survey's findings convert into working practice for those who build on these systems. Design against signatures, not devices: characterize each phase of your workload by the §2.2 dimensions—the same model can be compute-, bandwidth-, and capacity-bound in one request path (§2.5)—and expect the binding phase, not the average, to set cost. Respect the batch-one reality: if your service cannot aggregate admissible concurrency, the §2.5 arithmetic is your ceiling on any wide device, and your leverage lives in the serving layer (batching policy, KV residency, speculative decoding) before it lives in hardware selection (§9.5). Measure at the useful-result boundary—wake scope, fallback copies, queueing, and retries are where paper efficiency dies (§2.10, §4.1)—and audit portability by which narrow waist your assets attach to (framework, kernel DSL, exchange format), not by whether a port "runs" (§9.7). Above all, internalize F2: when your workload hits a wall, the correct next step is the test, not the catalog—and the test's most common honest answer is more software work.

## 12.6 Guidance for Platform Architects

For those who assemble fleets and platforms, the survey reduces to portfolio discipline. Evaluate at the pod and facility boundary—memory per coherent domain, bisection, power, cooling, failure blast radius (§7.1, §10.4)—and treat power procurement and supply allocation as first-class planning terms (§10.5). Below hyperscale volume, the record's default verdict is the flexible platform plus rented specialization: the consumption models that multiplied during the snapshot period (cloud tensor processors, hosted wafer-scale, appliances) let you satisfy condition 1–3 workloads without owning condition 4 (§7.5). Protect the software seams that preserve exit options—serving layer, framework waist, portable kernel tier (§9.7)—and price flexibility's option value explicitly when a specialized offer undercuts the GPU on your current workload: the offer is betting your workload holds still, and Chapter 2's churn evidence says it will not (§4.9, §6.4). Finally, measure utilization as the vector of §4.3 under your actual SLOs, never as occupancy—several of the survey's worked bounds show busy hardware delivering little.

## 12.7 Guidance for Hardware Selection

The selection procedure the survey supports is a sequence, not a matrix. (1) Establish the workload signatures and their stability horizon (Chapter 2). (2) Run the software ceiling honestly—measured, not estimated; the FlashAttention/vLLM magnitudes show what disciplined software recovers (§2.5). (3) If a residual bound remains, locate it in the §5.11 partition: absorbed bounds mean wait for the platform; structural bounds mean the specialized family is safe (NPU for wake scope, deterministic silicon for certification, near-sensor for locality); the contested middle means run the full §2.14 test with Appendix G's arithmetic. (4) Weight the software stack per Chapter 9's audit before any silicon comparison. (5) Prefer consumption to ownership until volume and stability prove otherwise. Per-class defaults fall out directly: hyperscale—internal or rented tensor processors where portfolios are stable, GPUs elsewhere; enterprise—GPUs plus consumption-model specialization; client—the SoC decides, coverage decides the SoC; embedded/automotive—the certified NPU tier, with determinism bought at the §6.4 end. None of these defaults is permanent; all of them are re-derivable from the test when conditions change, which is the point.

## 12.8 Research Gaps

The survey closes with what it could not resolve, stated as invitations. **The measurement gap**: public, comparable, system-boundary measurements—sustained thermal-equilibrium client performance, end-to-end energy per useful result including wake and fallback, utilization under realistic SLOs—remain scarce; MLPerf's families cover fractions of the need (§2.10, §8.2), and much of this survey's quantitative layer had to be author-computed from datasheets for exactly this reason. **The irregularity gap**: no family yet owns dynamic sparsity, routing, and graph traversal cleanly (§2.7, §6.8); the architecture that makes irregular memory access a first-class citizen at scale remains unbuilt. **The programming-model gap**: PIM and analog tiles await a portable abstraction (§6.8, §11.2, §11.4)—the narrow-waist question of §9.7 posed one level lower. **The certification gap**: proving learned components safe within bounded-time systems remains an open joint problem of architecture and verification (§8.5). **The economics gap**: condition 4 is the survey's decisive variable and its least publicly documented—real utilization, real TCO, real enablement costs are competitive secrets, leaving analyses like Appendix G's on illustrative arithmetic. Each gap is also a prediction: where measurement, abstraction, or proof is missing, the next structural change is being incubated.

## 12.9 Roadmap for Future Editions

The survey was built in stability layers (Charter §16), and its revision schedule should follow them. The workload taxonomy and contracts (Chapters 2–4) should be re-validated against model-family drift—§11.7's wildcards, especially persistent-state agents, are the likeliest forcers of a structural change such as a new family chapter. The family chapter (6) should absorb verdicts as the contested middle resolves further; the landscape chapters (7–8) age by design and warrant refresh each product cycle; the futures chapter (11) should have its evidence classes re-graded—the fate of §11.2's standardization bet and §11.3's package-optics watch item being the scheduled checkpoints. Quantum computing re-enters scope only on the evidence conditions of §11.8. The two-transition thesis itself is the layer this edition leaves most confident: it began as an organizing claim and ends as a demonstrated pattern with a mechanism (§12.3)—which is what a thesis is for.

## 12.10 Closing

Modern AI acceleration is best understood not as an account of chips but as a conversation between software and physics, transacted in economics. Software optimization succeeds until it exposes a bound; the bound is either absorbed by the incumbent platform, escaped by changing a contract, or lived with because the economics say so; and the deployment class decides which. That sentence is the survey. Everything else—the signatures, the contracts, the stressors, the families, the landscapes, the futures—is the machinery that makes it decidable rather than rhetorical, and the machinery, unlike any product this survey names, is built to last.
